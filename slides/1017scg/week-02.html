Introduction to Logarithms

A logarithm is the inverse of an exponent. It tells us what power we need to raise a base to in order to get a certain number.

If: \[ a^x = b \] Then: \[ \log_a b = x \]

  • \( \log_2 8 = 3 \) because \( 2^3 = 8 \)
  • \( \log_{10} 1000 = 3 \) because \( 10^3 = 1000 \)

Evaluating Logarithms Without a Calculator

Simplify the following by thinking in terms of powers:

  • \( \log_2 16 = 4 \) because \( 2^4 = 16 \)
  • \( \log_3 81 = 4 \) because \( 3^4 = 81 \)
  • \( \log_5 25 = 2 \) because \( 5^2 = 25 \)
  • \( \log_{10} 1 = 0 \) because \( 10^0 = 1 \)

Logarithm Laws

  • Product Law: \[ \log_a(xy) = \log_a x + \log_a y \]
  • Quotient Law: \[ \log_a\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_a x - \log_a y \]
  • Power Law: \[ \log_a(x^n) = n \log_a x \]

These rules work for any base \( a > 0 \), \( a \ne 1 \).

Using Log Laws â€“ Examples

  • \( \log_2(8 \cdot 4) = \log_2 8 + \log_2 4 = 3 + 2 = 5 \)
  • \( \log_3\left(\frac{81}{3}\right) = \log_3 81 - \log_3 3 = 4 - 1 = 3 \)
  • \( \log_5(25^3) = 3 \log_5 25 = 3 \times 2 = 6 \)

Change of Base Rule

If your calculator only has log base 10 or natural log (ln), you can use the change of base formula:

\[ \log_b a = \frac{\log_c a}{\log_c b} \] where \( c \) can be 10 (common log) or \( e \) (natural log).

Example: \[ \log_2 10 = \frac{\log_{10} 10}{\log_{10} 2} = \frac{1}{0.3010} \approx 3.32 \]
